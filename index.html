Product-Led Growth vs. Sales-Led Growth

Let the product sell itself — or build an army to sell it?

Product-led growth (PLG) means your product IS the primary acquisition, activation, and revenue driver. Users sign up, experience value, and upgrade — all without talking to a salesperson. Sales-led growth (SLG) means a sales team drives acquisition through demos, negotiations, and relationship-building. Most successful B2B companies use a hybrid, but the balance depends on product complexity, deal size, and buyer behaviour. The fundamental question: can a user experience enough value on their own to justify paying? If yes, PLG. If they need guidance, SLG. If both, hybrid.

How to apply it

Map your product against three PLG prerequisites: (1) Can users self-serve? If setup requires integration, configuration, or training, pure PLG won't work. (2) Is the value obvious quickly? Users need an "aha moment" within minutes, not weeks. (3) Is the decision-maker the user? If the buyer and user are different people (IT buys, employees use), you need sales to reach the buyer. Zoom nailed all three: anyone could start a meeting in 30 seconds (self-serve), the video quality was obviously better (quick value), and individual users could buy with a credit card (user = buyer). This is why PLG worked so well for Zoom.

Example

Zoom's GTM genius was using PLG for land (free users adopt Zoom bottom-up) and SLG for expand (sales team converts organisations to paid plans). A teacher starts using free Zoom for virtual classes. Students experience it. They use it at work. Their company has 500 employees on free Zoom. Now Zoom's sales team calls the CIO: "You already have 500 users. Want enterprise features — SSO, admin controls, compliance?" The sale is easy because adoption already happened. This "land and expand" model let Zoom grow from $60M to $622M revenue in two years (2018-2020) without the massive sales force that competitors like Cisco WebEx required.

GTM Motion Selection Framework

  1. Self-Serve Test — Can a user sign up, set up, and get value without human help? If yes: PLG viable. If no: need sales or customer success.
  2. Time-to-Value — How fast does a user reach the "aha" moment? Minutes: PLG. Days: PLG with onboarding. Weeks: sales-assisted. Months: enterprise sales.
  3. Buyer-User Alignment — Is the person who pays also the person who uses? Same person: PLG. Different people: need sales to bridge the gap.
  4. Deal Size — ACV under $5K: PLG (self-serve). $5K-$50K: PLG + inside sales. $50K-$500K: field sales. $500K+: enterprise sales + solutions engineering.
  5. Virality Potential — Does using the product naturally expose non-users? (Zoom meetings include non-users. Slack messages stay inside.) High virality: PLG advantage.
  6. Market Maturity — New category? Need sales to educate. Existing category with known need? PLG can work because users know what they want.
  7. Hybrid Timing — Most companies start PLG or SLG, then add the other. When to add sales to PLG: when you see enterprise usage but no conversion. When to add PLG to SLG: when you want to reduce CAC.

Pricing & Packaging Strategy

Price is a signal, not just a number.

Pricing is the most powerful lever in your GTM toolkit — and the most neglected. A 1% improvement in pricing yields 11% profit improvement (vs. 3.3% from volume and 7.8% from cost reduction). Your pricing communicates positioning (premium vs. accessible), segments your market (free vs. pro vs. enterprise), and shapes user behaviour (which features drive upgrades). The three pricing dimensions: the model (per-user, per-usage, flat-rate), the metric (what you charge for), and the level (how much). Get any one wrong and your GTM stalls.

How to apply it

Start with your value metric — the unit of value that correlates with how much value customers get. Zoom charges per host (not per participant) because the host is the person getting value from organising the meeting. Slack charges per active user because more active users = more value from communication. Your value metric should: (1) scale with customer value, (2) be easy to understand, (3) be predictable for the buyer. Then design packages around natural upgrade triggers: what limitation in the free plan creates enough friction that users want to pay, but not so much friction that they leave?

Example

Zoom's pricing evolution shows strategic mastery. The free plan had a brilliant constraint: unlimited 1-on-1 calls but a 40-minute limit on group calls. This was genius because: (1) 1-on-1 calls let users experience Zoom's quality advantage over Skype (builds habit), (2) group calls are where the real business value is (meetings, webinars), (3) the 40-minute limit creates natural friction ("sorry everyone, we need to restart the Zoom") that's embarrassing enough to trigger upgrades but not annoying enough to switch to a competitor. Pro at $14.99/host/month removed the limit. Business at $19.99 added admin features. Enterprise added dedicated support. Each tier mapped to a clear buyer persona and budget.

Pricing Strategy Framework

  1. Value Metric — What unit of value do you charge for? Per user, per host, per GB, per transaction? It should scale with the value your customer receives.
  2. Free Plan Design — What's free? Enough to demonstrate value. What's gated? The features that unlock the biggest value for paying use cases. The free plan is marketing, not charity.
  3. Upgrade Trigger — What moment makes a free user NEED to upgrade? (40-min limit, storage full, team size exceeded.) This should be a natural workflow friction, not an arbitrary wall.
  4. Package Structure — How many tiers? Usually 3-4. Each tier should have a clear buyer persona, a clear "why upgrade" from the tier below, and a meaningful price gap.
  5. Price Anchoring — Your highest tier makes your middle tier look reasonable. Price the top tier for your best customers, not for everyone.
  6. Annual vs. Monthly — Offer both. Annual discount (typically 15-20%) improves retention and cash flow. Monthly lowers the commitment barrier for new users.
  7. Competitive Positioning — Are you priced above, below, or at competitors? Below: need volume. Above: need differentiation. At parity: need a different axis to compete on.

Crossing the Chasm

The gap between early adopters and mainstream customers kills most products.

Geoffrey Moore's "Crossing the Chasm" describes the most dangerous moment in a product's GTM journey: the gap between early adopters (who buy because it's new) and the early majority (who buy because it works and others use it). Early adopters tolerate bugs, missing features, and incomplete experiences. The early majority demands reliability, integration with existing workflows, social proof, and low switching costs. The product, messaging, and sales motion that won early adopters will NOT win the majority. Most startups die in the chasm — beloved by a small group, ignored by the mass market.

How to apply it

To cross the chasm, pick a beachhead segment: one specific market segment where you can win completely and become the default. Don't try to serve the entire mainstream market at once. Win one segment decisively, then expand from that stronghold. Zoom's beachhead was small business meetings: teams of 5-50 people who needed reliable video calls without IT support. They didn't try to compete with Cisco for Fortune 500 enterprises or with Skype for consumer video calls. They owned the mid-market meeting use case, built word-of-mouth there, then expanded up-market (enterprise) and into adjacent use cases (webinars, phone, events).

Example

Zoom's chasm-crossing was textbook. In 2013-2016, Zoom was beloved by tech-savvy early adopters who valued video quality. But mainstream businesses still used WebEx and Skype because "that's what IT approved." Zoom crossed the chasm with three moves: (1) Beachhead: focused on small businesses that didn't have IT departments to block them — users could adopt Zoom without asking permission. (2) Whole product: added recording, scheduling integrations (Outlook, Google Calendar), and phone dial-in — features the mainstream demanded. (3) Social proof: publicized case studies from recognisable brands like Uber and Nasdaq. By 2018, Zoom had crossed the chasm: it was the default choice for new meeting tool purchases, not just the "cool alternative" for early adopters.

Chasm-Crossing Playbook

  1. Beachhead Selection — Pick ONE segment you can dominate. Criteria: urgent need, reachable, can win in 6-12 months, referenceable for adjacent segments.
  2. Whole Product Audit — What's missing between your product and the "whole product" the beachhead needs? (Integrations, support, documentation, training.)
  3. Compelling Reason to Buy — Early majority needs a REASON to switch, not just a better product. What business pain is urgent enough to overcome switching inertia?
  4. Social Proof — Early majority buys what others buy. Collect case studies, logos, reviews, and analyst recognition from your beachhead wins.
  5. Risk Reduction — Early majority is risk-averse. Offer free trials, money-back guarantees, migration support, and SLAs that reduce the perceived risk of switching.
  6. Channel Alignment — Are you selling through channels the early majority trusts? (Enterprise: Gartner, resellers. SMB: app marketplaces, online reviews.)
  7. Adjacent Expansion — Once you own the beachhead, which adjacent segment is closest? Expand segment by segment, not all at once.

Competitive Positioning & Market Timing

You don't win by being better. You win by being different.

In a crowded market, "better" is not a GTM strategy. When Zoom launched in 2013, there were already 10+ video conferencing tools. Zoom didn't win because it was "better WebEx" — it won because it was positioned differently: "video conferencing that just works." The positioning defined the message (simplicity), the audience (frustrated WebEx/Skype users), the channel (word of mouth from delighted users), and the competitive frame (us vs. the frustration of existing tools, not us vs. specific competitors). Great positioning makes selling easier because customers self-select into your product.

How to apply it

Use April Dunford's positioning framework: (1) Competitive alternatives — what would customers use if you didn't exist? (2) Unique attributes — what do you have that alternatives don't? (3) Value — what do those attributes enable? (4) Target customers — who cares most about that value? (5) Market category — what context makes your value obvious? Zoom's positioning: competitive alternative = WebEx/Skype. Unique attribute = reliability + simplicity + quality. Value = meetings start on time without IT headaches. Target = anyone who's ever said "can you hear me?" Market category = video conferencing (not "unified communications" — that's the incumbent's framing).

Example

Zoom's market timing was as important as its product quality. In 2013, three trends converged: (1) Bandwidth became cheap enough for reliable HD video. (2) Remote work was growing 10% YoY. (3) WebEx and Skype had become so frustrating that "can you hear me?" was a universal joke. Eric Yuan (ex-VP Engineering at WebEx) understood the frustration intimately and timed the launch perfectly. But timing alone isn't enough — Google Hangouts had the same timing and lost. Zoom combined timing with execution: freemium for adoption, obsessive focus on call quality, and a simple brand promise. The lesson: market timing creates the window, but positioning and execution determine who climbs through it.

Positioning & Timing Assessment

  1. Competitive Alternatives — What do customers use today? (Incumbent tools, manual workarounds, doing nothing.) This is your real competition.
  2. Unique Capabilities — What can you do that alternatives genuinely can't? Be honest. "Better" isn't unique. "Different" is.
  3. Value Translation — So what? Translate capabilities into outcomes customers care about. (Not "HD video" but "meetings that start on time.")
  4. Best-Fit Customers — Who gets the most value? Target them first. A product for everyone is positioned for no one.
  5. Market Category — What frame of reference makes your value obvious? Choose the category that positions your strengths as must-haves.
  6. Timing Signals — What technology, behaviour, or market shift creates the window? (Bandwidth, remote work, AI, regulation.) Is the window open NOW?
  7. Narrative Control — What story do you tell the market? The company that defines the narrative wins. (Zoom = "it just works" was a narrative, not a feature list.)
